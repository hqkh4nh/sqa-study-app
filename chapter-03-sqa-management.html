SQA Management

Software quality is conformance to explicit requirements, documented standards, and implicit expectations. This chapter covers SQA activities, quality reviews, defect amplification, the formal technical review (FTR), and software reliability.

01 — What Quality Is

What is Software Quality?

Simplistically, quality is an attribute of software that implies the software meets its specification. This definition is too simple for ensuring quality in software systems, because:

  • software specifications are often incomplete or ambiguous;
  • some quality attributes are difficult to specify;
  • tension exists between some quality attributes, e.g. efficiency vs. reliability.
Definition

Software quality is conformance to explicitly stated functional and performance requirements, explicitly documented development standards, and implicit characteristics that are expected of all professionally developed software.

  • Software requirements are the foundation from which quality is measured — lack of conformance to requirements is lack of quality.
  • Specified standards define a set of development criteria that guide the manner in which software is engineered — if the criteria are not met, lack of quality will almost surely result.
  • Implicit requirements often go unmentioned — if software conforms to its explicit requirements but fails to meet its implicit requirements, software quality is suspect.

02 — Ensuring Quality

Software Quality Assurance — SQA

To ensure quality in a software product, an organization must have a three-prong approach to quality management:

  1. Organization-wide policies, procedures and standards must be established.
  2. Project-specific policies, procedures and standards must be tailored from the organization-wide templates.
  3. Quality must be controlled — the organization must ensure that the appropriate procedures are followed for each project.

Standards exist to help an organization draft an appropriate software quality assurance plan — e.g. ISO 9000-3 and ANSI/IEEE standards. External entities can be contracted to verify that an organization is standard-compliant.

03 — The Activities

SQA Activities

  • Applying technical methods — to help the analyst achieve a high quality specification and a high quality design.
  • Conducting formal technical reviews — a stylized meeting conducted by technical staff with the sole purpose of uncovering quality problems.
  • Testing software — a series of test case design methods that help ensure effective error detection.
  • Enforcing standards.
  • Controlling change — applied during software development and maintenance.
  • Measurement — track software quality and assess the ability of methodological and procedural changes to improve software quality.
  • Record keeping and reporting — provide procedures for the collection and dissemination of SQA information.

04 — The Case For & Against

SQA Advantages & Disadvantages

Advantages

Fewer latent defects (reduced testing & maintenance effort); higher reliability → greater customer satisfaction; maintenance costs can be reduced; overall life cycle cost of software is reduced.

Disadvantages

Difficult to institute in small organizations (resources are not available); represents cultural change — and change is never easy; requires the expenditure of dollars that would not otherwise be budgeted.

05 — Reviews

Quality Reviews

The fundamental method of validating the quality of a product or a process. Applied during and/or at the end of each life cycle phase, in order to:

  • point out needed improvements in the product of a single person or team;
  • confirm those parts of a product in which improvement is either not desired or not needed;
  • achieve technical work of more uniform, or at least more predictable, quality.

Quality reviews can have different intents: review for defect removal, review for progress assessment, and review for consistency and conformance.

The relative cost of finding and fixing a defect rises dramatically across the life cycle — each phase is paired with its review:

06 — Cost Impact

Defect Amplification and Removal

Each development step receives errors from previous steps and passes errors to the next step. Within a step the model distinguishes:

  • Errors passed through — defects entering from the prior step that are not detected here;
  • Amplified errors (1:x) — existing defects that spawn additional defects;
  • Newly generated errors — defects introduced in this step.

A percent efficiency for error detection is applied at each step. Worked through the slides' example, an early design defect amplifies to 94 errors entering integration; with 50% detection efficiency at integration, validation and system testing, 12 latent errors remain — illustrating why finding defects early (with reviews) is so valuable.

08 — The FTR

Formal Technical Review (FTR)

A software quality assurance activity performed by software engineering practitioners, whose objectives are to uncover errors in function, logic or implementation; verify that the software meets its requirements; assure it has been represented according to predefined standards; achieve software developed in a uniform manner; and make projects more manageable.

FTR is actually a class of reviews: walkthroughs, inspections, round-robin reviews, and other small group technical assessments of the software.

The Review Meeting

Constraints

Between 3 and 5 people (typically); advance preparation should involve no more than 2 hours of work per person; duration should be less than two hours.

Components

Product (component to be reviewed), Producer (who developed it), Review leader (evaluates readiness, distributes materials), Reviewers (spend 1–2 hours reviewing), Recorder (records all important issues raised).

Review Reporting and Recordkeeping

Review Summary Report

What was reviewed? Who reviewed it? What were the findings and conclusions?

Review Issues List

Identifies the problem areas within the product, and serves as an action item checklist that guides the producer as corrections are made.

Guidelines for FTR

Key guidelines include: review the product, not the producer; set an agenda and maintain it; limit debate and rebuttal; enunciate problem areas but don't attempt to solve every problem; take written notes; limit participants and insist upon advance preparation; develop a checklist for each product; and conduct meaningful training for all reviewers.

Reviewer's Preparation

Understand the context of the material; skim all product material to understand its location and format; then read and annotate a hardcopy; pose your written comments as questions; avoid issues of style; and inform the review leader if you cannot prepare.

Results of Review Meeting

All attendees of the FTR must make a decision:

  • Accept the product without further modification;
  • Reject the product due to severe errors (and perform another review after corrections);
  • Accept provisionally — minor corrections are needed, but no further reviews are required.

A sign-off is completed, indicating participation and concurrence with the review team's findings.

09 — Reliability

Software Reliability

Definition

Software reliability: the probability of failure-free operation for a specified time in a specified environment.

This is not an objective measure — it must be based on an operational profile, and there are widely varying consequences for different errors.

Software Reliability Improvements

  • Reliability improves when faults present in the most frequently used portions of the software are removed.
  • A removal of X% of faults doesn't necessarily mean an X% improvement in reliability — in a study by Mills et al. (1987), removing 60% of faults resulted in only a 3% improvement in reliability.
  • Removing faults with the most serious consequences is the primary objective.

10 — Takeaways

Summary

Quality must be planned, built in, measured, and reviewed — not added at the end.

SQA management establishes organization-wide and project-tailored standards, controls that they are followed, applies technical methods and formal technical reviews, tracks quality through measurement, and recognizes that reliability is driven by removing the faults that matter most.

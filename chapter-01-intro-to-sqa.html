Introduction to Software Quality Assurance

Quality software does not just happen — it has to be planned, measured, and built into the development process. This chapter reviews the people, process, product and project view of quality, software metrics, reviews, and software configuration management.

01 — Foundations

The Four P's in Software Development

The central question of the chapter is: “How can we achieve software quality?” Quality emerges from the interaction of four P's:

  • Process — a set of activities; provides a template for the project, guided by measurement & feedback.
  • People — the software engineers who participate in the project; developed through education & training.
  • Product — the set of artifacts that result from the project; validated through testing & measurement.
  • Project — the work itself, kept on track through management & monitoring.

02 — What SQA Is

Software Quality Assurance

Definition — E.H. Bersoff

“Quality assurance consists of those procedures, techniques, and tools applied by professionals to ensure that a product meets or exceeds pre-specified standards during it's development cycle.”

— E.H. Bersoff

It follows that SQA:

  • is an essential activity for any business that produces products used by others;
  • needs to be planned and systematic — it does not just happen;
  • needs to be built into the development process — a natural outcome of software engineering;
  • has continuous improvement as its overall goal.

SQA — an umbrella activity

Quality sits at the centre of six supporting activities: Methods and Tools, Standards and Procedures, Metrics and Measurement, Formal Technical Reviews, Software Configuration Management, and Testing.

Why SQA activities pay off

The cost to find and fix a defect rises sharply across the lifecycle — SQA pays off in the early phases because defects are cheap to fix:

03 — Quality & Principles

Software Quality

What is software quality, and how do we measure it?

  • customer's viewpoint → meets specifications
  • developer's viewpoint → easy to maintain, test, …
Software quality is not just about meeting specifications and removing defects! Other attributes also affect quality:
safetyunderstandabilityportability
securitytestabilityusability
reliabilityadaptabilityreusability
resiliencemodularityefficiency
robustnesscomplexitylearnability

We need to select critical quality attributes early in the development process and plan for how to achieve them.

Principles of Software Quality Assurance

  1. We have a set of standards and quality attributes that a software product must meet. → There is a goal to achieve.
  2. We can measure the quality of a software product. (key point) → There is a way to determine how well the product conforms.
  3. We track the values of the quality attributes. → It is possible to assess how well we are doing.
  4. We use information about software quality to improve future products. → There is feedback into the development process.

04 — Standards, Metrics & Formal Approaches

Software Standards

Product standards

define the characteristics all product artifacts should exhibit so as to have quality.

Process standards

define how the software process should be conducted to ensure quality software.

Standards matter because they encapsulate best practices, provide a framework around which to implement the SQA process, and assist in ensuring continuity of project work. Each project decides which standards should be ignored, used as is, modified, or created.

Software Metrics

Definition

metric: any type of measurement that relates to a software system, process or related artifact.

Control metrics

used to plan, manage and control the development process (e.g., effort expended, elapsed time, disk usage).

Predictor metrics

used to predict an associated product quality (e.g., cyclomatic complexity can predict ease of maintenance).

External attribute

something we can only discover after the software has been put into use (e.g., ease of maintenance).

Internal attribute

something we can measure directly from the software itself (e.g., cyclomatic complexity).

We want to use internal attributes to predict the value of external attributes.

Product quality: design quality metrics

For a design component, the key quality attribute is maintainability, which is related to:

  • cohesion — how closely related is the functionality of the component?
  • coupling — how independent is the component?
  • understandability — how easy is it to understand what the component does?
  • adaptability — how easy is it to change the component?

Most of these cannot be measured directly, so we infer a relationship with a component's complexity. Using fan-in / fan-out:

  • fan-in — number of calls to a component by other components → high fan-in ⇒ high coupling.
  • fan-out — number of components called by a component → high fan-out ⇒ high complexity.
complexity = component-length x (fan-in x fan-out)²

Other measures: the design structure quality index (DSQI) (0–1, from IEEE Std 982.1-1988) and the software maturity index (SMI) (0–1) — as SMI approaches 1, the product begins to stabilize.

Product quality: formal approaches

  • Proving programs/specifications correct — logically prove requirements were correctly transformed into programs.
  • Statistical Quality Assurance — categorize defects and find causes; the 80-20 rule: 80% of defects can be traced to 20% of causes.
  • The Cleanroom Process — a combination of the above two approaches.

05 — Reviews

Project Quality: Reviews

Reviews are the principal method of validating the quality of a project, and lead to early discovery of defects — each phase is paired with a walkthrough or review (requirements walkthroughs, analysis walkthroughs, design walkthroughs, code walkthroughs, test plan review).

Requirements, Analysis and Design introduce 50–60% of all defects — and formal technical reviews can uncover 75% of these!

06 — Configuration Management

Software Configuration Management (SCM)

SCM is an activity executed throughout the system life cycle to control change of products and life cycle artifacts. Its activities are identify, control, audit, report, and support.

Definition

configuration item: an artifact to which we want to control changes. Each must be identified and described (unique name, type, project identifier, change/version information, resources, and a pointer to the actual item).

Baselines

Definition

A baseline is a time/phase in the software development (usually a project milestone) after which any changes must be formalized (e.g., go through a formal change control procedure).

To become a baseline, a configuration item must first pass a set of formal review procedures and join the project software library; afterwards a check-out procedure controls access and change.

Version control

version

itemk is obtained by modifying itemi (bug fixes, enhanced functionality); created in a linear order.

branch

a concurrent development path requiring independent configuration management.

variant

different configurations intended to coexist (e.g., Oracle for Windows / Oracle for Linux).

change control

a change request is evaluated by a change control authority, which issues an engineering change order if approved.

Audit, status reporting & support

  • Audit — ensures that changes have been properly implemented (have the proper steps and procedures been followed?).
  • Status reporting — keeps all parties informed and up-to-date on the status of a change.

07 — Process & People

Process Quality

Does process quality ⇒ product quality? Software development has unique factors that affect quality: software is designed, not manufactured; development is creative, not mechanical; individual skills and experience have significant influence; and external factors (novelty, commercial pressure) intrude. So processes are organization specific, people and technology may matter more than process, and insufficient resources always hurt quality.

ISO 9001 / 9000-3 and the SEI CMM

The ISO 9000 quality model is a generic quality model, instantiated as an organizational quality process — its focus is on process management (certification is easy and can be a marketing ploy). The SEI Capability Maturity Model (CMM) helps an organization improve its development processes — its focus is on process improvement:

LevelCMM process
1Initial process (ad hoc)
2Repeatable process (intuitive)
3Defined process (qualitative)
4Managed process (quantitative)
5Optimizing process

People Quality: People CMM (PCMM)

The People Capability Maturity Model (PCMM) is intended to improve the knowledge and skill of people — its focus is on people improvement — across the same five levels (Initial, Repeatable, Defined, Managed, Optimizing).

08 — Takeaways

Summary — Software Quality

Quality software does not just happen!

Quality assurance mechanisms should be built into the software development process. Developing quality software requires management support and involvement, the gathering and use of software metrics, policies and procedures that everyone follows, and commitment to following them even when things get rough.

Remember

Testing is an important part of quality assurance, but it's not all there is to obtaining a quality software product.
